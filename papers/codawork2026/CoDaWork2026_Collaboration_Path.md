## Step 7 → Paper: Two Open Theorems

The v3.0 paper contains two results that are empirically established but formally unproven. These are not speculations — they are patterns observed across 25 experiments that a CoDa mathematician can prove or disprove.

**Theorem A: EITT (Entropy Invariance under Temporal Transformation)**

*Conjecture:* For any compositional dataset 𝒳 on the simplex 𝒮ᴰ, geometric-mean decimation preserves Shannon entropy to within a bounded variation:

|ℋ(𝒟ₖ(𝒳)) − ℋ(𝒳)| / ℋ(𝒳) < ε for k = 2, 4, 8

*Evidence:* 25 experiments, 18 domains, 44 orders of magnitude. Every natural system passes. The only failures involve extreme carrier hierarchy (5+ orders of magnitude between largest and smallest carrier).

*What a proof looks like:* The geometric mean is the Aitchison centre — the natural centre of the simplex. Decimation by geometric-mean blocks is subsampling via the Aitchison barycenter. A proof would show that barycentric subsampling on 𝒮ᴰ preserves Shannon entropy as a consequence of the geometry. The tools exist in Aitchison's own framework.

*What a disproof looks like:* A compositional dataset where geometric-mean decimation systematically shifts entropy. This would be equally publishable — it identifies the boundary condition.

**Theorem B: Transcendental Resonance — Architecture or Physics?**

*Conjecture:* The variance trajectory σ²(t) locks to transcendental constants (within δ < 0.01) at a rate 39× higher than random compositions.

*Open experiment:* Replace CLR with ILR. If the same constants match under ILR, the resonance is intrinsic to the simplex geometry. If the matches shift, the resonance is an artifact of the CLR embedding.

*Why this matters to CoDa:* The CLR/ILR choice is a foundational debate in CoDa. This experiment produces new evidence on what the two transforms preserve and what they distort. Either outcome is a contribution.

Each theorem is a standalone paper. Together with the v3.0 instrument description, they form a three-paper package.
