## The Five Stages

### Stage 1: CoDaWork 2026 — Community Introduction (June 2026)

**Goal:** Engagement, not endorsement. Leave Coimbra with collaborators.

**Success criteria:**
- 2–3 researchers agree to run their own data through Hˢ independently
- Someone takes the EITT proof challenge seriously
- Someone proposes or agrees to run the ILR vs CLR test
- The talk generates productive disagreement, not polite silence

**What you have:** Complete talk (15 min + 5 min Q&A), 12-slide deck, interactive cosmic slider demo, 5-minute Q&A loop animation, public repo, honest framing of weak claims.

**What you do NOT claim:** That the transcendental resonance is physically meaningful. That EITT is proven. That the pipeline has agency. You report detections, not established physics. You ask questions, not rhetorical ones.

**Language that survives peer review:**
- "The instrument exhibits structural attractor behaviour in informational space" (not "it has agency")
- "The Aitchison geometry acts as a structural attractor for conservation laws" (not "the simplex wants to show you conservation laws")
- "Point of interest" (not "discovery")
- "Detection" (not "proof")

### Stage 2: Peer-Reviewed Publication (Target: Q4 2026 – Q2 2027)

**Two papers minimum:**

**Paper 1 — The Instrument Paper**
- What: Hˢ pipeline specification, 12 steps, 4 guards, deterministic guarantee, conjugate preservation proof (3 theorems + 1 corollary), EITT empirical result, 78 diagnostic codes, fingerprint system
- Target journals: Mathematical Geosciences (Pawlowsky-Glahn's home), Computational Statistics and Data Analysis, or the Journal of the Royal Statistical Society
- Does NOT claim transcendental resonance is physically meaningful
- Establishes the pipeline as a valid measurement instrument

**Paper 2 — The Applications Paper**
- What: 25 experiments, 18 domains, cross-domain fingerprint comparison, cosmic energy budget as centrepiece, power map, conservation laws on the simplex
- Co-authored with CoDa collaborators who ran independent validation
- Target: same journal, or broader venue if cosmic result generates interest

**What you need that you don't have yet:**
- Independent replication: at least 3 experiments run by someone else, on data you didn't choose, producing results you didn't predict in advance
- A CoDa co-author who gives the paper credibility inside the community
- CoDaWork is where you find both

**Timeline:** 12–18 months from submission to publication. Faster with CoDa insider co-author.

### Stage 3: The EITT Proof (Timeline: Unknown — depends on mathematicians)

**What changes:** If a mathematician proves that geometric-mean decimation preserves Shannon entropy on the Aitchison simplex, that's a theorem. Published in a pure mathematics journal. Becomes the theoretical foundation the instrument paper rests on.

**What you do:** Make the conjecture precise. State exact conditions. Provide 25 empirical data points. Define "geometric-mean decimation" in terms of the Aitchison inner product. Then wait.

**What you cannot do:** Write this paper yourself. You said so in the speech. That honesty is an asset.

---

### Stage 4: ISO Standardisation (Target: 3–5 years post-publication)

**Path:** National standards body (Standards Council of Canada, SCC) → ISO Technical Committee

**Relevant ISO committees:**
- TC 69: Applications of statistical methods
- TC 176: Quality management (if Hˢ framed as QA instrument for compositional data)

**Requirements:**
- Published standard method (peer-reviewed instrument paper)
- Evidence of use by multiple independent organisations
- Demonstrated need for standardisation (enough users that interoperability matters)
- National body willing to sponsor the work item

**Shortcut:** ISO 17025 (general requirements for competence of testing and calibration laboratories). Hˢ largely meets 17025 already: determinism, traceability, input guards, audit trail, gauge R&R. Any lab can adopt it under 17025 without waiting for a dedicated ISO standard.

---

### Stage 5: Metrology Recognition (Target: 5–10 years)

**Institutions:**
- BIPM (Bureau International des Poids et Mesures) — international coordination
- NRC (National Research Council of Canada) — Canadian national metrology institute
- NIST (US), PTB (Germany) — if international collaborators emerge

**Requirements:**
- Everything above, plus:
- Measurement uncertainty budget
- Calibration protocol (Hs-21 Reference Standards is the seed)
- Traceability to SI units or defined reference (the Aitchison metric)
- International recognition of the simplex as a measurement domain

**This is not discouraging.** This is the normal pace for a new measurement methodology entering the international system.

### Networking Targets

**Who to find:**

- **Anyone working on entropy and the simplex.** They can evaluate the EITT analytic bound. Ask: "Have you encountered entropy preservation under subsampling in your work?"
- **ILR researchers.** They can run the CLR vs ILR test. Ask: "If I gave you a pipeline that uses CLR, would you be willing to replicate it with ILR and compare?"
- **Geochemistry practitioners.** They have canonical CoDa benchmark datasets (Aar granite, Coxite, etc.). Ask: "Would you be willing to run one of your standard datasets through Hˢ and tell me what it finds?"
- **Anyone who asks a hard question during Q&A.** They cared enough to engage. Find them at coffee. Thank them for the question. Ask what they work on.

**Who to avoid:**
- Nobody. This is a small community. Everyone matters. Even sceptics are useful — they tell you where the weak points are.

| "My pipeline found conservation laws" | "The ratio-pair analysis detected stability at machine epsilon" |
