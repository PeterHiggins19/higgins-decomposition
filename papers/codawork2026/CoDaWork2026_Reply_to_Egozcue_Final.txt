Dear Prof. Egozcue,

Thank you very much for taking the time to read my proposal and for
your thoughtful comments. I also appreciate you sharing your paper on
subcompositional coherence — I have read it carefully and it has
sharpened my thinking on several points. Let me respond to each of
your queries.

(1) Entropy invariance.

You are right to flag this. Shannon entropy is not scale invariant,
and I should not have implied it was an Aitchison-geometric property.
What I observe empirically is narrower: when the closed composition
is temporally decimated (every 2nd, 4th, 8th year), the entropy of
the closed composition changes very little. This is a statement about
the smoothness of the trajectory on the simplex, not about entropy
being a proper CoDa functional. I will present it as an empirical
observation about trajectory robustness, not as a geometric theorem.
I note your work with Pawlowsky-Glahn on evidence information as the
proper scale-invariant alternative, and I would welcome guidance on
whether that measure could replace Shannon entropy in this role.

(2) CLR versus ILR for classification.

Thank you for your encouraging answer. I use CLR throughout the
pipeline because it preserves the one-to-one correspondence between
components and carriers, which matters for interpretation in the
energy domain. As you note, the Aitchison distances are the same in
either coordinate system, so the classification should survive the
substitution. Your warning about amalgamation not commuting with
perturbation is important — in my pipeline, amalgamation is always
applied to the raw composition before any centering or transformation,
precisely to avoid this ordering problem. I will make the order of
operations explicit in the presentation.

(3) Independent validation.

I understand that the CoDa community works primarily in R. The
pipeline is written in Python, but the underlying operations are
standard CoDa computations — closure, CLR, variation matrix, Aitchison
distance — that can be reproduced in any language. I will make the
data and the step-by-step methodology available so that R users can
verify the results independently. The live demonstration at the end
of the talk runs in a web browser and requires no software installation.

Regarding the abstract: I would like to proceed with the original
scope — compositional monitoring of energy-mix drift on the simplex —
with one update. EMBER has released their 2025 Global Electricity
Review, and the energy landscape has shifted significantly. Renewables
overtook coal generation globally for the first time, and the countries
in my study (Germany, Japan, United Kingdom) have all moved
substantially. I have recomputed the full pipeline with the updated
dataset through 2025 using the current version of the system. Same
method, same scope, fresh data. The results will be current as of
the conference.

I look forward to presenting in Coimbra and to learning from the
community. If you have any further suggestions, I am happy to
incorporate them.
